A Gap Between Beliefs and Actions

[I] Parents carry the important responsibility of guiding children toward independence, yet recent findings show a striking gap between what adults say they value and what they actually allow their children to experience. [II] Many parents of younger children claim they want their kids to manage tasks on their own; however, far fewer report that their children routinely speak with healthcare providers, make simple financial choices, or place their own orders in public. [III] Although most agree that unsupervised free time is beneficial, only a portion of these children spend time at home alone, move through stores independently, or meet friends without adult supervision. [IV]

A major barrier appears to be worry. Many parents hesitate because they fear their child could be harmed, followed, or frightened by others‚Äîeven though only a small percentage believe their neighborhood is unsafe. Some adults cite concerns about immaturity, potential judgment from others, or even misunderstandings about local laws. These factors create a "blame culture" in which parents feel pressure to supervise constantly, making them less willing to give their children the gradual, low-risk experiences necessary for building autonomy and responsibility. As a result, children may miss important opportunities to practice communication, decision-making, and self-management.

Research consistently shows that independence strengthens confidence, resilience, and mental health, and parents play a critical role in guiding these early steps. However, many unintentionally restrict progress by continuing to perform tasks children can manage themselves. This pattern resembles "helicopter parenting," where constant involvement, though well-intentioned, limits a child's chance to develop competence through real-world practice. Experts suggest that rising anxiety levels in young people may be linked to a lack of such experiences.

To bridge the gap between beliefs and behaviors, parents can start by asking whether there is a good reason to prevent a child from doing a task independently. From there, they can create purposeful opportunities during everyday routines‚Äîmeal preparation, simple errands, or interacting with trusted adults in public spaces. Choosing calm moments rather than rushed situations gives children the space to try, make mistakes, and learn. Over time, stepping back thoughtfully allows children to gain the experience needed to become confident, capable, and self-reliant.
